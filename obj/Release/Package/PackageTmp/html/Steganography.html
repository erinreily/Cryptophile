Steganography

Steganography was the precursor to cryptography. While cryptography makes a message difficult to read without knowing the cipher, steganography simply hides the message. This works to a certain degree; as long as no one but the intended recipient knows about the message, the secret is safe, but if the existence of the message is revealed, anyone can read it. In ancient history, people hid their messages in various ways. One way was to tattoo the message onto a shaven head, let the hair regrow, and shave it again when the carrier reached the destination. Another was to etch a message into a tablet or other such surface and cover it with wax.


Although steganography has its drawbacks, it does have an advantage over cryptography. Where an encrypted message might draw attention and interest--thereby increasing the chances of it being broken--a concealed message is easily overlooked. Modern steganographic techniques include making text the same color as the background in documents/emails/web pages.
