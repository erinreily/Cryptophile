Monoalphabetic Substitution (Caesar Cipher)

Substitution ciphers work by replacing each letter of the plain text with another letter. The Caesar Cipher, used by its namesake, Julius Caesar, is a type of monoalphabetic substitution cipher, and works by "shifting" each letter by a certain number of places down the alphabet. For example, if you have a shift of 2, and your plain text is ABC, then your encrypted text would be CDE. Shifting by 2 would mean: your letter A would become C, your letter B would become D, and your letter C would become E.


This cipher, though simple to use, is ineffective in today's world as there are only 26 possible ways to decipher a message encoded in such a way. Once someone knows that a message is Caesar Cipher, it's only a matter of time (and a short time at that) to figure out the meaning.
